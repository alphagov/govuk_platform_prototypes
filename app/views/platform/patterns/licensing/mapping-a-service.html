Map existing service

To transform a service, is it useful to know exactly what is being transformed.

When going from Discovery to Alpha, having a single, agreed version of how service currently works, helps to inform how that service should be designed.

When to map a existing service

Mapping a service is not a rule

A map is just a way to organise information a team has discovered about it's users and service.

Only having performed a Discovery, should a team consider mapping how it's service currently works.

The activity of mapping a service can become a distraction from learning about your user and service. Only map the current service when:

  • your users' needs are known
  • your service's needs are known
  • deciding what and how to measure service performance
  • you need data to show the impact of potential design decisions
  • a single
  • you are ready to share your understanding of the current service, before exploring how to design it.

What to map

Having the right parts of service mapped, can help ensure decisions about the design of a service are as informed as possible.

Your team should map:

  • user needs
  • The beginning, middle and end of the current service
  • the people involved using and delivering the service
  • each individual stage in using and delivering the service

If you do make a map, work hard to keep it clear and simple. It can be tempting to map every type of thing included in a service. Maps with too many types of information can say so much, they say nothing.

Your team can also map:

  • Information needed to do each step
  • Actions involved during each step
  • Channels used during each step
  • Technology for storing and sharing information for each step

Mapping the existing costs

Before using the licensing pattern to help design a new service, the same pattern can help teams work out the cost of the existing service.

Cost per transaction is useful data but being more specific

What are the existing service's:

  • cost per user to discover or not discover your service
  • cost of routing per users
  • cost of checking eligibility per user
  • cost of deciding suitability per user
  • cost of issuing a licence per user

How to map

A map should be readable to anyone, even if they are not involved or familiar with your service.

Using tried and tested formats for describing parts of a service, is a way of ensuring a service map is readable by others across government.

Advice:

  • Use a wall
  • Put in a public space
  • Invite others views of the map
  • Iterate the map
  • Aim for a single, agreed version of the map
